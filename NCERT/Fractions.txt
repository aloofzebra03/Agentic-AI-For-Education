### Concept: Fraction as equal share

#### Description
Imagine you have something delicious, like a pizza, and you want to share it equally among your friends. A fraction is just a way to talk about these equal shares or parts of a whole. It tells us how many parts we have out of the total number of equal parts the whole thing was divided into.

#### Key Topics from the Textbook
*   Representing a whole as a fraction.
*   Representing parts of a whole as fractions.
*   Understanding that the denominator represents the total number of equal parts.
*   Understanding that the numerator represents the number of parts taken or considered.

#### Detail
When we talk about "fraction as equal share," we're focusing on the idea that the whole thing is divided into parts that are exactly the same size.

*   **The Whole:** This is whatever we are dividing. It could be a cake, a chocolate bar, a group of toys, or even a whole day.
*   **The Equal Shares:** The whole is broken down into smaller pieces. The crucial part is that all these pieces must be of the same size.
*   **The Fraction:** A fraction is written as two numbers separated by a line (e.g., 1/2, 3/4).
    *   The **denominator** (the bottom number) tells us how many equal shares the whole was divided into.
    *   The **numerator** (the top number) tells us how many of those equal shares we are talking about, have, or are considering.

#### Working
Let's say you have 1 chocolate bar, and you want to share it equally among 4 friends.

1.  **Identify the Whole:** The whole is the 1 chocolate bar.
2.  **Divide into Equal Shares:** You break the chocolate bar into 4 pieces of the exact same size. Each piece is an equal share.
3.  **Determine the Denominator:** Since you divided the chocolate bar into 4 equal pieces, the denominator of your fraction will be 4.
4.  **Determine the Numerator:** If you give one piece to your first friend, you are considering 1 of those 4 equal shares. So, the numerator is 1.
5.  **Write the Fraction:** The fraction representing one share is 1/4. Each friend gets 1/4 of the chocolate bar. If you wanted to talk about what two friends get together, it would be 2/4.

#### Intuition / Logical Flow
Think about a pizza. A whole pizza is like "1". If you cut it into 8 equal slices, each slice is an "equal share".
*   If you take 1 slice, you have 1 out of 8 equal slices. This is written as 1/8.
*   If you take 3 slices, you have 3 out of 8 equal slices. This is written as 3/8.
The denominator (8) tells you how many equal slices make up the whole pizza. The numerator (1 or 3) tells you how many of those slices you have. The concept of "equal share" is key because if the slices weren't equal, we couldn't simply use fractions to describe them.

#### Critical Thinking
If you have a packet of 12 marbles and you want to share them equally among 3 friends, what fraction of the marbles does *each* friend receive? How can you be sure that your division represents "equal shares"?

4.  You have a rope of length 2 meters. You cut it into 4 equal pieces. What fraction of the original rope is each piece?
    a) 4/2
    b) 1/2
    c) 1/4
    d) 2/4

#### Real-Life Applications
1.  **Sharing Food:** Cutting a cake, a pizza, or a bar of chocolate into equal slices for family or friends.
2.  **Measuring Ingredients:** When a recipe calls for "1/2 cup of flour," it implies dividing a standard cup into two equal parts and taking one.
3.  **Time Division:** Dividing an hour into equal minutes (e.g., 15 minutes is 1/4 of an hour).

#### Relation Between Sub-Concepts
The concept of "fraction as equal share" is the foundational idea upon which many other fraction concepts are built.
*   It directly leads to understanding **proper fractions** (where the numerator is less than the denominator, meaning you have less than a whole).
*   It's essential for visualising and comparing fractions. If you know 1/4 of a pizza is one equal slice, you can then compare it to 1/8 of a pizza (which is a smaller share).
*   It sets the stage for understanding equivalent fractions (e.g., 2/4 is the same amount as 1/2, meaning two equal smaller shares are the same as one equal larger share).

### Concept: Fractional Units

#### Description
Fractional units are parts of a whole. Just like you can have one whole apple, you can also have parts of an apple, like half an apple or a quarter of an apple. Fractions help us represent these parts precisely.

#### Key Topics from the Textbook
*   Understanding fractions as parts of a whole.
*   Numerator and Denominator.
*   Types of fractions (Proper, Improper, Mixed).
*   Equivalent fractions.
*   Comparing fractions.
*   Adding and subtracting fractions with like and unlike denominators.
*   Multiplying fractions.
*   Dividing fractions.

#### Detail
*   **Fraction:** A fraction is written as a ratio of two numbers, separated by a line. For example, $\frac{1}{2}$ or $\frac{3}{4}$.
*   **Numerator:** The top number in a fraction. It tells us how many parts we have.
*   **Denominator:** The bottom number in a fraction. It tells us how many equal parts the whole is divided into.

#### Working
Imagine a pizza cut into 8 equal slices.
*   If you eat 2 slices, you have eaten $\frac{2}{8}$ of the pizza. Here, 2 is the numerator (slices you ate) and 8 is the denominator (total slices the pizza was cut into).
*   If you give 3 slices to your friend, they have $\frac{3}{8}$ of the pizza.

#### Intuition / Logical Flow
Think about sharing! If you have a chocolate bar and you break it into 4 equal pieces, each piece is $\frac{1}{4}$ of the whole bar. If you take 3 of those pieces, you have $\frac{3}{4}$ of the chocolate bar. The denominator tells you how many pieces you broke it into, and the numerator tells you how many pieces you are considering.

#### Critical Thinking
If you have a collection of 20 marbles and $\frac{2}{5}$ of them are blue, how many marbles are blue?

#### Open-Ended MCQs

    a) $\frac{5}{3}$

    c) $\frac{1}{5}$
    d) $\frac{3}{10}$

2.  A cake is cut into 6 equal pieces. If you eat 1 piece, what fraction of the cake did you eat?
    a) $\frac{1}{5}$
    b) $\frac{6}{1}$
    c) $\frac{1}{6}$
    d) $\frac{5}{6}$

3.  In the fraction $\frac{7}{9}$, what does the number 9 represent?
    a) The number of parts taken.
    b) The total number of equal parts in the whole.
    c) The value of each part.
    d) The remainder after subtraction.

#### Real-Life Applications
1.  **Cooking:** Recipes often use fractions for ingredients (e.g., $\frac{1}{2}$ cup of flour, $\frac{3}{4}$ teaspoon of salt).
2.  **Time:** We talk about "half an hour" or "a quarter past the hour," which are fractional units of time.
3.  **Measurement:** When measuring lengths or distances, we use fractions (e.g., $3\frac{1}{2}$ inches).

#### Relation Between Sub-Concepts
*   **Numerator and Denominator:** These are the fundamental building blocks of any fraction. The relationship between them defines what the fraction represents.
*   **Types of Fractions:** Proper fractions (numerator < denominator) represent less than a whole, improper fractions (numerator $\ge$ denominator) represent a whole or more, and mixed fractions combine a whole number with a proper fraction.
*   **Equivalent Fractions:** Different fractions can represent the same fractional part of a whole (e.g., $\frac{1}{2} = \frac{2}{4}$). This is achieved by multiplying or dividing both the numerator and denominator by the same non-zero number.
*   **Comparing Fractions:** Understanding the values of numerators and denominators helps us determine which fraction is larger or smaller.
*   **Operations on Fractions:** Adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing fractions all rely on understanding what each fractional unit represents and how to combine or divide these units.

### Concept: Reading Fractions

#### Description
Reading fractions is all about understanding what a fraction represents. A fraction is a way to show a part of a whole thing. When we read a fraction, we are telling the number of parts we have and how many equal parts the whole thing was divided into.

#### Key Topics from the Textbook
*   Understanding the numerator and denominator.
*   Reading fractions using "out of" or "parts per".

#### Detail
A fraction has two parts:
1.  **Numerator:** The top number. It tells us how many parts we have.
2.  **Denominator:** The bottom number. It tells us the total number of equal parts the whole is divided into.

For example, in the fraction $\frac{3}{4}$:
*   The numerator is 3.
*   The denominator is 4.

This means we have 3 parts out of a whole that has been divided into 4 equal parts.

#### Working
To read a fraction, follow these steps:
1.  **Identify the Denominator:** Look at the bottom number. This tells you how many equal parts the whole is divided into. You will read this number as "fourths", "fifths", "sixths", and so on.
2.  **Identify the Numerator:** Look at the top number. This tells you how many of those equal parts you are considering. You will read this number as its usual value (one, two, three, etc.).
3.  **Combine:** Put them together. For example, if the fraction is $\frac{2}{5}$, you read it as "two-fifths" or "two out of five equal parts".

#### Intuition / Logical Flow
Imagine you have a pizza cut into 8 equal slices. If you eat 3 slices, you have eaten $\frac{3}{8}$ of the pizza. You are reading it as "three-eighths" because you have 3 slices (numerator) out of a total of 8 equal slices (denominator) that the pizza was cut into.

#### Critical Thinking
If you see a fraction like $\frac{7}{10}$, how would you explain to someone who has never seen fractions before what this means in terms of dividing a cake?

#### Open-Ended MCQs

    c) The value of each part.

2.  How would you read the fraction $\frac{9}{10}$ aloud?
    a) Nine-tens.
    b) Nine out of ten.
    c) Nine-tenths.

3.  In a class of 30 students, 11 are girls. What fraction of the class are girls, and how would you read it?
    a) $\frac{11}{30}$ - Eleven-thirtieths.
    b) $\frac{30}{11}$ - Thirty-eleven.
    c) $\frac{11}{30}$ - Eleven out of thirty.
    d) Both a and c.

#### Real-Life Applications
1.  **Cooking:** When a recipe calls for $\frac{1}{2}$ cup of flour, you are reading a fraction to measure an amount.
2.  **Sharing:** If you share a chocolate bar into 4 equal pieces and eat 1, you've eaten $\frac{1}{4}$ (one-fourth) of the bar.
3.  **Time:** If you have 30 minutes left out of an hour, you have $\frac{30}{60}$ or $\frac{1}{2}$ (half) of an hour.

#### Relation Between Sub-Concepts
The numerator and denominator are the two fundamental parts of a fraction. Reading a fraction involves understanding the distinct role of each: the denominator tells you the "size" of the parts (how many in the whole), and the numerator tells you "how many" of those parts you are considering.

### Concept: Numerator

#### Description
The numerator is the **top number** in a fraction. It tells us **how many parts** of the whole we have or are considering.

#### Key Topics from the Textbook
*   Parts of a Fraction: Numerator, Denominator, Fraction Bar.

#### Detail
A fraction is a way to represent a part of a whole. It is written in the form of $\frac{\text{Numerator}}{\text{Denominator}}$.
*   The **Numerator** is the number above the fraction bar.
*   The **Denominator** is the number below the fraction bar.
*   The **Fraction Bar** is the line separating the numerator and the denominator.

For example, in the fraction $\frac{3}{4}$:
*   The numerator is 3.
*   The denominator is 4.
This fraction means we have 3 parts out of a total of 4 equal parts.

#### Working
To understand the numerator, imagine a pizza cut into equal slices.
1.  **Identify the whole:** The entire pizza is the whole.
2.  **Divide into equal parts:** The pizza is cut into a certain number of equal slices. This number is the **denominator**.
3.  **Count the considered parts:** If you take some slices, the number of slices you have taken is the **numerator**.

Example: If a pizza is cut into 8 equal slices (denominator = 8) and you eat 3 of them, the fraction representing the eaten part is $\frac{3}{8}$. Here, the numerator is 3.

#### Intuition / Logical Flow
Think of the numerator as "how much you *have*" or "how many you *took*". The denominator tells you "how many *there are in total*". So, if you see a fraction, the top number (numerator) is always about the specific quantity you are interested in, out of the total.

#### Critical Thinking
If you have a fraction representing the number of boys in a class, and the fraction is $\frac{15}{25}$, what does the numerator 15 tell you about the class? What would happen to the numerator if 5 more boys joined the class, assuming the total number of students also increased accordingly?

2.  If a cake is divided into 12 equal slices and you have 5 slices, which number is the numerator in the fraction representing the cake you have?
    a) 12

3.  Which of the following fractions has a numerator of 9?
    a) $\frac{5}{9}$
    b) $\frac{9}{15}$
    c) $\frac{1}{9}$
    d) $\frac{9}{9}$

#### Real-Life Applications
1.  **Sharing Food:** If a chocolate bar is divided into 10 equal pieces and you eat 3, the fraction you ate is $\frac{3}{10}$. The numerator (3) tells you how many pieces you ate.
2.  **Recipes:** A recipe might call for $\frac{2}{3}$ cup of flour. The numerator (2) tells you how many thirds of a cup are needed.
3.  **Scores:** If a test has 25 questions and you answered 20 correctly, your score can be represented as $\frac{20}{25}$. The numerator (20) shows how many questions you got right.

#### Relation Between Sub-Concepts
The numerator is intrinsically linked to the **denominator**. The denominator defines the total number of equal parts that make up the whole. The numerator then tells us how many of *those specific parts* (as defined by the denominator) we are focusing on. Without a denominator, the numerator alone doesn't provide enough information to understand what fraction of a whole is being represented.

### Concept: Denominator

#### Description
The denominator is the bottom number in a fraction. It tells us into how many equal parts a whole has been divided.

#### Key Topics from the Textbook
*   Understanding the parts of a fraction (numerator and denominator).
*   Meaning of the denominator in terms of equal parts.

#### Detail
A fraction represents a part of a whole. It is written as $\frac{Numerator}{Denominator}$. The **denominator** is the number below the line. It's like the "name" of the parts. For example, in the fraction $\frac{3}{4}$, the denominator is 4. This means the whole is divided into 4 equal parts.

#### Working
When you see a fraction, the denominator tells you the total number of identical pieces the whole is broken into. For instance, if you have $\frac{1}{2}$, the denominator '2' means the whole is divided into 2 equal pieces. If you have $\frac{2}{5}$, the denominator '5' means the whole is divided into 5 equal pieces.

### Concept: Mixed Fractions

#### Description
A mixed fraction is a way to write a number that has a whole number part and a fractional part. Think of it like having some whole cookies and then a piece of another cookie. It's a convenient way to represent quantities that are more than one whole but not a whole number of wholes.

#### Key Topics from the Textbook
*   Understanding mixed fractions as a combination of a whole number and a proper fraction.
*   Converting improper fractions to mixed fractions.
*   Converting mixed fractions to improper fractions.

#### Detail
A mixed fraction has two parts:
1.  **Whole Number Part:** This is the integer part of the number. For example, in $3\frac{1}{2}$, '3' is the whole number part.
2.  **Fractional Part:** This is a proper fraction, meaning the numerator is smaller than the denominator. For example, in $3\frac{1}{2}$, '$\frac{1}{2}$' is the fractional part.

So, $3\frac{1}{2}$ means 3 whole things and half of another thing.

#### Working

**1. Converting Improper Fractions to Mixed Fractions:**
An improper fraction is a fraction where the numerator is greater than or equal to the denominator (e.g., $\frac{7}{3}$). To convert it to a mixed fraction:
*   **Divide the numerator by the denominator.**
*   The **quotient** becomes the **whole number part**.
*   The **remainder** becomes the **numerator** of the fractional part.
*   The **denominator** stays the **same**.

*Example:* Convert $\frac{7}{3}$ to a mixed fraction.
   *   Divide 7 by 3: $7 \div 3 = 2$ with a remainder of $1$.
   *   Quotient = 2 (whole number part)
   *   Remainder = 1 (numerator of the fractional part)
   *   Denominator = 3 (stays the same)
   *   So, $\frac{7}{3} = 2\frac{1}{3}$.

**2. Converting Mixed Fractions to Improper Fractions:**
To convert a mixed fraction (e.g., $2\frac{1}{3}$) back to an improper fraction:
*   **Multiply the whole number part by the denominator of the fractional part.**
*   **Add the numerator of the fractional part** to the result from the previous step. This will be your new numerator.
*   The **denominator stays the same**.

*Example:* Convert $2\frac{1}{3}$ to an improper fraction.
   *   Multiply the whole number (2) by the denominator (3): $2 \times 3 = 6$.
   *   Add the numerator (1): $6 + 1 = 7$. This is the new numerator.
   *   The denominator is 3 (stays the same).
   *   So, $2\frac{1}{3} = \frac{7}{3}$.

#### Intuition / Logical Flow
Imagine you have 7 cookies that you want to divide equally among 3 friends.
*   Each friend gets 2 whole cookies ($7 \div 3 = 2$ with a remainder of 1).
*   You have 1 cookie left over to share among the 3 friends. So, each friend gets an additional $\frac{1}{3}$ of a cookie.
*   Therefore, each friend gets $2$ whole cookies and $\frac{1}{3}$ of another cookie, which we write as $2\frac{1}{3}$. This is the same as having 7 thirds of a cookie ($\frac{7}{3}$).

### Concept: Number line

#### Description
A number line is a straight line that shows numbers. It helps us to understand and compare numbers, especially whole numbers, integers, and sometimes fractions. Think of it as a ruler, but instead of just inches or centimeters, it shows all the numbers in order.

#### Key Topics from the Textbook
*   Representation of natural numbers on a number line.
*   Representation of whole numbers on a number line.
*   Representation of integers (positive and negative) on a number line.
*   Addition of integers on a number line.
*   Subtraction of integers on a number line.

#### Detail
A number line typically has a central point marked as '0'. Numbers increase as you move to the right of '0' (positive numbers) and decrease as you move to the left of '0' (negative numbers). The distance between any two consecutive numbers on the line is always the same.

#### Working
1.  **Drawing a Number Line:** Draw a straight line. Mark a point as '0'.
2.  **Marking Positive Numbers:** Mark points to the right of '0' at equal distances, labeling them 1, 2, 3, and so on.
3.  **Marking Negative Numbers:** Mark points to the left of '0' at equal distances, labeling them -1, -2, -3, and so on.
4.  **Representing Operations:**
    *   **Addition:** To add a positive number, move to the right. To add a negative number, move to the left.
    *   **Subtraction:** To subtract a positive number, move to the left. To subtract a negative number, move to the right.

#### Intuition / Logical Flow
Imagine you are standing at a specific point on the number line. If you need to add something positive (like money), you move forward (to the right). If you need to add something negative (like debt), you move backward (to the left). Similarly, if you need to take away something positive, you move backward (to the left), and if you need to take away something negative (which is like gaining something), you move forward (to the right).

### Concept: Equivalent Fractions

#### Description
Equivalent fractions are like different ways of saying the same amount or the same part of a whole. They look different because they have different numbers on the top and bottom, but when you simplify them or represent them visually, they represent the exact same value. Think of them as different names for the same delicious pizza slice!

#### Key Topics from the Textbook
*   Identifying equivalent fractions.
*   Generating equivalent fractions by multiplying or dividing the numerator and denominator by the same non-zero number.

#### Detail
Fractions represent a part of a whole. A fraction has two parts: the **numerator** (the top number) which tells us how many parts we have, and the **denominator** (the bottom number) which tells us how many equal parts the whole is divided into.

Equivalent fractions have the same value. For example, 1/2 and 2/4 are equivalent fractions. If you cut a pizza into 2 equal slices and take 1, that's half the pizza. If you cut the same pizza into 4 equal slices and take 2, you still have half the pizza!

#### Working
To find equivalent fractions, you can perform two main operations:

1.  **Multiplication:** Multiply both the numerator and the denominator of a fraction by the **same non-zero number**. This creates a new fraction that represents the same value.
    *   Example: To find an equivalent fraction for 1/3, you can multiply both the numerator and denominator by 2: (1 Ã— 2) / (3 Ã— 2) = 2/6. So, 1/3 is equivalent to 2/6.
    *   You can also multiply by 3: (1 Ã— 3) / (3 Ã— 3) = 3/9. So, 1/3 is equivalent to 2/6 and 3/9.

2.  **Division:** Divide both the numerator and the denominator of a fraction by the **same non-zero number** that is a common factor of both. This is also called simplifying a fraction.
    *   Example: To find an equivalent fraction for 4/8, you can divide both the numerator and denominator by 2: (4 Ã· 2) / (8 Ã· 2) = 2/4. So, 4/8 is equivalent to 2/4.
    *   You can divide 4/8 by 4: (4 Ã· 4) / (8 Ã· 4) = 1/2. So, 4/8 is equivalent to 2/4 and 1/2.

#### Intuition / Logical Flow
Imagine a chocolate bar.

*   If you have **1/2** of the bar, you have one out of two equal pieces.
*   If the bar is broken into **4 equal pieces**, and you have **2 of them (2/4)**, you still have the same amount of chocolate. You just cut your original pieces in half.
*   If the bar is broken into **6 equal pieces**, and you have **3 of them (3/6)**, you still have the same amount.

The underlying logic is that when you multiply or divide both parts of the fraction by the same number, you are essentially scaling the number of pieces and the number of parts you have proportionally. If you double the total number of slices (denominator), you must also double the number of slices you take (numerator) to keep the amount the same.

### Concept: Lowest Terms

#### Description
Lowest terms, also known as simplest form, is a way to write a fraction so that its numerator and denominator have no common factors other than 1. It's like simplifying a fraction to its most basic and reduced version.

#### Key Topics from the Textbook
*   Fractions in lowest terms
*   Simplifying fractions

#### Detail
When we talk about a fraction being in lowest terms, it means we've divided both the top number (numerator) and the bottom number (denominator) by the largest possible common factor. This makes the fraction easier to understand and work with, without changing its actual value.

#### Working
To simplify a fraction to its lowest terms, you need to:
1.  **Find the Greatest Common Divisor (GCD)** of the numerator and the denominator. The GCD is the largest number that divides both of them without leaving a remainder.
2.  **Divide** both the numerator and the denominator by their GCD.

**Example:** Simplify the fraction $\frac{12}{18}$
1.  **Find GCD:** The factors of 12 are 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12. The factors of 18 are 1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18. The common factors are 1, 2, 3, 6. The greatest common divisor (GCD) is 6.
2.  **Divide:**
    *   $12 \div 6 = 2$
    *   $18 \div 6 = 3$
    So, $\frac{12}{18}$ in its lowest terms is $\frac{2}{3}$.

#### Intuition / Logical Flow
Think of it like sharing cookies. If you have 12 cookies and want to divide them equally among 18 friends, it's complicated to figure out each person's share directly. But if you simplify the problem by first grouping the cookies and friends, you might find a simpler way. $\frac{12}{18}$ means 12 parts out of 18. If you can group these parts into larger equal chunks, you get a simpler representation. For example, you can group the 12 cookies into 2 groups of 6, and the 18 friends into 3 groups of 6. Now, each of the 3 larger groups gets 2 cookies, which is $\frac{2}{3}$. The overall share is the same, but it's easier to grasp.

### Concept: Brahmagupta's Method for Adding Fractions

#### Description
Brahmagupta's method is a clever way to add two fractions without needing to find a common denominator right away. It's like a shortcut that helps us quickly find the sum of two fractions.

#### Key Topics from the Textbook
*   Adding two fractions using Brahmagupta's method.

#### Detail
Brahmagupta's method works for adding two fractions, say $\frac{a}{b}$ and $\frac{c}{d}$. The method involves multiplying the "opposite" numerators and denominators and then adding them.

#### Working
To add two fractions $\frac{a}{b}$ and $\frac{c}{d}$ using Brahmagupta's method, follow these steps:

1.  **Multiply the numerator of the first fraction by the denominator of the second fraction:** $a \times d$
2.  **Multiply the numerator of the second fraction by the denominator of the first fraction:** $c \times b$
3.  **Add the results from steps 1 and 2:** $(a \times d) + (c \times b)$ This will be the numerator of your sum.
4.  **Multiply the denominators of the two fractions:** $b \times d$ This will be the denominator of your sum.

So, the sum of $\frac{a}{b}$ and $\frac{c}{d}$ is $\frac{(a \times d) + (c \times b)}{b \times d}$.

**Example:** Add $\frac{2}{3}$ and $\frac{1}{4}$.
1.  $a=2, b=3, c=1, d=4$
2.  Step 1: $a \times d = 2 \times 4 = 8$
3.  Step 2: $c \times b = 1 \times 3 = 3$
4.  Step 3: $(a \times d) + (c \times b) = 8 + 3 = 11$ (This is the new numerator)
5.  Step 4: $b \times d = 3 \times 4 = 12$ (This is the new denominator)

Therefore, $\frac{2}{3} + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{11}{12}$.

#### Intuition / Logical Flow
Think of it like this: when you add $\frac{a}{b}$ and $\frac{c}{d}$, you want to make both fractions have the same "size" of parts (a common denominator). Brahmagupta's method does this implicitly. For $\frac{a}{b}$, you're essentially scaling it up by multiplying its numerator and denominator by $d$ (making it $\frac{ad}{bd}$). For $\frac{c}{d}$, you're scaling it up by multiplying its numerator and denominator by $b$ (making it $\frac{cb}{db}$). Now that they both have the same denominator ($bd$), you can just add their numerators ($ad + cb$).

### Concept: Brahmagupta's method for subtracting fractions

#### Description
Brahmagupta's method is a clever way to subtract fractions that helps us find the difference between two fractions without needing to make their denominators the same immediately. It's like a shortcut that simplifies the subtraction process.

#### Key Topics from the Textbook
*   Subtraction of fractions with unlike denominators using Brahmagupta's method.
*   Cross-multiplication for subtracting fractions.

#### Detail
Brahmagupta's method for subtracting fractions, say $\frac{a}{b}$ and $\frac{c}{d}$, involves multiplying the numerator of the first fraction by the denominator of the second fraction, and subtracting from it the product of the numerator of the second fraction by the denominator of the first fraction. This difference becomes the new numerator. The new denominator is found by multiplying the denominators of the two original fractions.

So, the formula is:
$\frac{a}{b} - \frac{c}{d} = \frac{(a \times d) - (c \times b)}{b \times d}$

#### Working
Let's subtract $\frac{2}{3}$ from $\frac{5}{4}$ using Brahmagupta's method:

**Step 1:** Identify the numerators and denominators.
Here, $a=5$, $b=3$, $c=2$, and $d=4$.

**Step 2:** Calculate the new numerator.
New numerator = $(a \times d) - (c \times b) = (5 \times 4) - (2 \times 3) = 20 - 6 = 14$.

**Step 3:** Calculate the new denominator.
New denominator = $b \times d = 3 \times 4 = 12$.

**Step 4:** Write the resulting fraction.
The difference is $\frac{14}{12}$.

**Step 5:** Simplify the fraction (if possible).
We can simplify $\frac{14}{12}$ by dividing both numerator and denominator by their greatest common divisor, which is 2.
$\frac{14 \div 2}{12 \div 2} = \frac{7}{6}$.

So, $\frac{5}{4} - \frac{2}{3} = \frac{7}{6}$.

#### Intuition / Logical Flow
Think of it like this: we want to find out "how much more" $\frac{a}{b}$ is than $\frac{c}{d}$.
When we multiply $a$ by $d$ ($a \times d$), we're essentially scaling the first fraction's "part" to be out of a common "whole" size represented by $b \times d$.
Similarly, when we multiply $c$ by $b$ ($c \times b$), we're scaling the second fraction's "part" to also be out of the same common "whole" size ($b \times d$).
Now that both fractions are considered out of the same sized "whole" (which is $b \times d$), we can simply subtract their scaled numerators to find the difference.
